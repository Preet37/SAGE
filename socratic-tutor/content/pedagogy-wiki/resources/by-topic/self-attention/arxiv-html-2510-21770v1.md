Numerical Fragility in Transformers: A Layer-wise Theory for Explaining, Forecasting, and Mitigating Instability
Jinwoo Baek Department of Computer Science, Oregon State University
Abstract
We study numerical instability in Transformers trained in low precision and give a first-order, module-wise theory that predicts when and where forward errors amplify. For self-attention we derive a layer-wise bound that factors into three interpretable diagnostics: a score-scale ratio , a row-wise softmax sensitivity , and the value conditioning . We prove a residual relaxation inequality showing that residual blocks attenuate depth-wise accumulation, and we give a precision- and width-aware LayerNorm indicator with a corresponding first-order error bound in the -dominated regime. These pieces combine into a unified forward-stability bound whose per-layer right-hand side is directly estimable during training.
On Tiny-ViT/CIFAR-10, we evaluate the bound and its components in three studies. (Exp-1) A decisive scatter shows that the combined predictor tracks the observed FP32 LP forward mismatch across seeds, widths, and precisions; scaling by collapses mixed-precision points. (Exp-2) The time series of the maximal acts as an early-warning signal, consistently leading increases in forward error by 16–24 steps with correlations of 0.65–0.82 (permutation-test ) and a Precision@K of 0.89–1.00. (Exp-3) Guided by , a simple LayerNorm- intervention that targets yields small but consistent stabilization (e.g., mean tail-loss change at , cap ), with no architectural changes and negligible overhead.
Overall, our theory provides actionable, unitless diagnostics that (i) explain when self-attention is numerically fragile, (ii) forecast instability in advance, and (iii) motivate a minimally invasive mitigation that trends beneficial in practice.
1 Introduction
While training Transformers in low precision (FP16, BF16; hereafter LP) has become standard practice, even well-tuned, widely used recipes can exhibit forward-pass numerical errors and training instabilities. This is partly because popular heuristics—such as mixed-precision schedules, loss scaling, and LayerNorm tweaks111Mixed-precision schedule: a fixed casting/accumulation policy (e.g., FP16/BF16 compute with FP32 accumulation and FP32 master weights). Loss scaling: multiply the loss by a factor before backpropagation (and rescale updates) to avoid FP16 gradient underflow; dynamic schemes adjust on overflow. LayerNorm tweaks: modify the additive in to prevent division-by-zero and cancellation.—mitigate failures but are not predictive of when or where amplification occurs.
We explain the mechanisms that govern instability and present diagnostics that predict their loci and onset. For self-attention, we derive a layer-wise first-order forward-error bound whose right-hand side decomposes into three terms: a score-scale ratio , a row-wise softmax sensitivity , and a value-conditioning term (see Sec. [3](https://arxiv.org/html/2510.21770v1#S3)). We prove a residual relaxation inequality showing that residual connections attenuate depthwise accumulation via per-layer factors with . We further identify a precision- and width-aware LayerNorm indicator that marks entry into the -dominated regime and yields a simple first-order LayerNorm error bound (Sec. [3](https://arxiv.org/html/2510.21770v1#S3)). Together these yield a unified depth- stability bound with per-layer quantities directly estimable during training.

5 Related Work
Numerical precision and mixed-precision training.
Floating–point rounding and stability have long been studied in numerical analysis (Higham,, [2002](https://arxiv.org/html/2510.21770v1#bib.bib6); Goldberg,, [1991](https://arxiv.org/html/2510.21770v1#bib.bib4); IEEE Computer Society,, [2019](https://arxiv.org/html/2510.21770v1#bib.bib7)). In deep learning, mixed-precision training with dynamic loss scaling popularized practical FP16/BF16 training while maintaining accuracy (Micikevicius et al.,, [2018](https://arxiv.org/html/2510.21770v1#bib.bib9); Kalamkar et al.,, [2019](https://arxiv.org/html/2510.21770v1#bib.bib8)). Kernel-level techniques also improve both speed and numerical behavior; e.g., FlashAttention uses an IO-aware tiling scheme with online normalization that yields an exactly equivalent yet more numerically stable softmax accumulation (Dao et al.,, [2022](https://arxiv.org/html/2510.21770v1#bib.bib2)). Our work is complementary: rather than proposing a new kernel or recipe, we provide a first-order, per-layer error predictor that explains and forecasts where forward errors amplify under LP.
Transformers, attention, and normalization.
Transformers (Vaswani et al.,, [2017](https://arxiv.org/html/2510.21770v1#bib.bib10)) (and ViT-style vision models (Dosovitskiy et al.,, [2021](https://arxiv.org/html/2510.21770v1#bib.bib3))) rely heavily on softmax attention and LayerNorm (Ba et al.,, [2016](https://arxiv.org/html/2510.21770v1#bib.bib1)). Numerous works observed that architectural choices around normalization affect stability; pre-LN improves optimization and mitigates gradient pathologies relative to post-LN (Xiong et al.,, [2020](https://arxiv.org/html/2510.21770v1#bib.bib11)). Alternatives such as RMSNorm adjust the normalization operator while often retaining empirical stability (Zhang and Sennrich,, [2019](https://arxiv.org/html/2510.21770v1#bib.bib12)). Our analysis isolates three attention-side diagnostics—score-scale ratio, row-wise softmax sensitivity, and value conditioning—and a precision/width-aware LayerNorm indicator , tying these directly to forward error via first-order bounds.
Residual connections and stability.
Residual networks (He et al.,, [2016](https://arxiv.org/html/2510.21770v1#bib.bib5)) are known to ease optimization and improve conditioning of deep compositions. We formalize a residual relaxation perspective showing that under a small-gain condition, per-layer forward errors accumulate sub-multiplicatively through factors . This connects classical conditioning arguments to a training-time, module-wise forward-error view for Transformers.
Forecasting instability.
Most prior work detects instability via global signals (loss spikes, gradient norms) or resorts to conservative heuristics (loss scaling, larger in LN). To our knowledge, there is no prior per-layer early-warning diagnostic for forward mismatch with the lead–lag and Precision@K properties we document. Our finding that peaks of consistently precede increases in forward error provides a lightweight indicator that can be logged online.
6 Limitations
Our study has several limitations. (i) Scale and scope: Experiments use Tiny-ViT on CIFAR-10; while the theory is module-wise, we have not validated on very large-scale LMs or long-sequence regimes. (ii) First-order analysis: Bounds are first-order in ; kernel-dependent constants are absorbed and second-order interactions are dropped, which may under- or over-estimate risk in corner cases. (iii) Diagnostics vs. surrogates: For efficiency we sometimes use Frobenius-norm surrogates (e.g., for ) and truncated SVD; this can loosen constants in the predictor. (iv) Hardware/runtime dependence: Real kernels differ across vendors and drivers; while our –scaled collapse is robust across FP16/BF16 in our setup, absolute constants may shift elsewhere. (v) Mitigation strength: The LayerNorm- intervention is intentionally minimal; effect sizes on tail loss are small (as expected from first-order sensitivity) and we did not tune for downstream accuracy trade-offs or combine with other safeguards (e.g., gradient clipping or kernel changes). (vi) Metrics: We focus on a forward-mismatch metric ; extending to task metrics and long-horizon stability under distribution shift is future work.
7 Conclusion
We presented a layer-wise, first-order theory for forward stability in Transformers that (i) explains cross-precision mismatch via interpretable attention and LayerNorm diagnostics, (ii) forecasts instability using a simple early-warning signal ( lead–lag 16–24 steps; high Precision@K), and (iii) mitigates risk with a minimally invasive, precision/width-aware LayerNorm- adjustment guided by . On Tiny-ViT/CIFAR-10, the –scaled predictor collapses BF16/FP16 and aligns linearly with observed forward mismatch, while the intervention trends beneficial with negligible overhead.
We see two immediate directions: (1) scaling the diagnostics to large-scale LMs and long-sequence vision models, and (2) closing the loop by using the predictor to schedule mitigations (adaptive LN-, selective kernel choices) only when and where the early-warning signal fires. More broadly, we hope our module-wise perspective bridges numerical analysis and deep-learning practice, enabling principled, low-overhead stability tooling during training.
