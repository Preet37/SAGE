## Core Definitions

**Text-to-video (T2V).** Text-to-video generation is the task of producing a sequence of frames (a video) conditioned on a text prompt, typically using a generative model trained on paired (or weakly paired) text–video data; modern systems often extend image diffusion to video by modeling *space + time* jointly so that motion and appearance remain coherent across frames (Ho et al., *Video Diffusion Models*, NeurIPS 2022; OpenAI “Video generation models as world simulators” report).

**Temporal attention.** Temporal attention is an attention mechanism applied *across frames* (time dimension) so the model can relate content at time \(t\) to content at other times, improving temporal coherence. In Ho et al. (2022), temporal attention is inserted into a factorized space-time architecture by treating **H×W as batch** and attending over **frames**, using **relative position embeddings**; it can be **masked** to prevent mixing (enabling joint image+video training) (Ho et al., NeurIPS 2022).

**Temporal consistency.** Temporal consistency is the property that identities (subjects), backgrounds, and visual style remain stable across frames without flicker or sudden changes, while motion evolves smoothly. VBench operationalizes temporal consistency via dimensions like **subject consistency**, **background consistency**, **temporal flickering**, and **motion smoothness** with specific automated proxies (VBench++ / VBench).

**Sora.** Sora is OpenAI’s text-conditional diffusion model trained jointly on videos and images of variable durations, resolutions, and aspect ratios, using a transformer that operates on **spacetime patches** of compressed latent video codes (“patch tokens”), enabling scaling to longer, higher-fidelity generations (OpenAI “Video generation models as world simulators”; see also Weng’s diffusion-video overview image referencing Sora’s DiT-style patch processing).

**NeRF (Neural Radiance Field).** NeRF represents a scene as a continuous 5D function \(F_\theta(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{d})\to(\mathbf{c},\sigma)\) mapping 3D position \(\mathbf{x}\) and viewing direction \(\mathbf{d}\) to color \(\mathbf{c}\) and volume density \(\sigma\); images are rendered by integrating (alpha-compositing) predicted color and density along camera rays (MIT Vision Book Ch.45; NeRF equation summaries in the provided NeRF explainer cards).

**3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS).** 3D Gaussian Splatting represents a scene explicitly as a set of anisotropic 3D Gaussians (with center, covariance, opacity, and color—often view-dependent) and renders them via differentiable rasterization by projecting Gaussians to screen-space ellipses and alpha-compositing them in depth order; it is designed for real-time rendering and fast optimization compared to NeRF-style ray marching (Kerbl et al. repo summary; 3DGS vs NeRF for real-time SLAM/telepresence card).

**World models.** A world model is a generative model trained (often unsupervised) to learn a compressed spatial and temporal representation of an environment, enabling an agent to plan or learn policies using features from the model—or even train inside “hallucinated dreams” generated by the model and transfer to the real environment (Ha & Schmidhuber, “World Models”, 2018). In this lesson context, large video generators are often discussed as stepping toward world-model-like simulation (OpenAI Sora report framing).

## Key Formulas & Empirical Results

### Video diffusion (Ho et al., 2022): forward process, objective, guidance, and temporal architecture anchors
- **Forward diffusion (continuous-time form, Eq. 1):**
  \[
  q(z_t|x)=\mathcal N(z_t;\alpha_t x,\sigma_t^2 I),\quad
  q(z_t|z_s)=\mathcal N\!\left(z_t;\frac{\alpha_t}{\alpha_s}z_s,\sigma_{t|s}^2 I\right)
  \]
  - Supports: “video diffusion is the same diffusion math as images, but on spatiotemporal tensors.”

- **Training objective (weighted denoising MSE, Eq. 2):**
  \[
  \mathbb E_{\epsilon,t}\big[w(\lambda_t)\|\hat x_\theta(z_t)-x\|_2^2\big]
  \]
  with **\(\epsilon\)-prediction**:
  \[
  \hat x_\theta(z_t)=\frac{z_t-\sigma_t\epsilon_\theta(z_t)}{\alpha_t}
  \]
  - Supports: “the model learns to denoise noisy video latents.”

- **Classifier-free guidance (Eq. 6):**
  \[
  \tilde\epsilon_\theta(z_t,c)=(1+w)\epsilon_\theta(z_t,c)-w\epsilon_\theta(z_t)
  \]
  - Supports: “prompt adherence can be increased at sampling time.”

- **Architecture detail (Section 3):** factorized 3D U-Net uses **space-only 3D convs** (e.g., \(1\times3\times3\)) + **temporal attention** over frames (relative position embeddings). Temporal attention can be **masked** to treat frames as independent images, enabling joint image+video training.

- **Joint image+video training metric gains (Section 4.3.1):** appending **0/4/8** independent image frames to each video (with masked temporal attention) improved **FVD** on 16×64×64 text-to-video from **202.28 → 68.11 → 57.84**, and **FID-avg** from **37.52 → 18.62 → 15.57** (Ho et al., 2022).
  - Supports: “mixing image data can materially improve video diffusion quality.”

- **Reconstruction-guided conditional sampling (Eq. 7–8):** adds gradient guidance to avoid incoherence in conditional generation/extension. For SR conditioning (Eq. 8):
  \[
  \tilde x_\theta(z_t)=\hat x_\theta(z_t)-w_r\frac{\alpha_t}{2}\nabla_{z_t}\|x_a-\hat x^a_\theta(z_t)\|_2^2
  \]
  - Supports: “conditioning/extension can be made coherent by guiding toward reconstruction of the known part.”

- **Coherence improvement for long extension (Table 6):** extending to **64 frames** from a 16-frame model: with guidance weight 2.0, **FVD 136.22** (reconstruction guidance) vs **451.45** (replacement); similarly at 5.0: **133.92** vs **456.24** (Ho et al., 2022).
  - Supports: “replacement-style block conditioning causes temporal incoherence; reconstruction guidance fixes it.”

### VBench / VBench++: temporal-consistency metrics and evaluation protocol anchors
- **Temporal Quality dimensions (VBench++):**
  - **Subject Consistency:** DINO feature similarity across frames.
  - **Background Consistency:** CLIP feature similarity across frames.
  - **Temporal Flickering:** mean absolute difference across (static) frames.
  - **Motion Smoothness:** motion priors from a video frame interpolation model.
  - **Dynamic Degree:** RAFT optical flow magnitude.
- **Prompt suite scale (VBench++):** ~**100 prompts per dimension**; plus **8 categories × 100 prompts** = **800** prompts.
- **Human preference protocol (VBench++):** per prompt: **4 models** generate videos; sampled into **5 groups**; each group does **6 pairwise comparisons**; annotators judge **only the target dimension**.

### NeRF: core rendering discretization (MIT Vision Book; NeRF equation cards)
- **Discrete alpha-compositing form (MIT Vision Book Ch.45):**
  \[
  \ell(r)\approx \sum_{i=1}^{T}\alpha_i\,(1-e^{-L^\sigma(\mathbf R_i)\delta_i})\,L^c(\mathbf R_i,\mathbf D)
  \]
  where \(\alpha_i=\exp(-\sum_{j<i}L^\sigma(\mathbf R_j)\delta_j)\), \(\delta_i=t_{i+1}-t_i\).
  - Supports: “NeRF rendering is weighted accumulation along rays; weights come from density and transmittance.”

### 3DGS projection math anchor (EWA splatting classic)
- **Perspective-projected footprint variance (Zwicker et al., 2002; Eq. 31–33):**
  \[
  V'_k = J_k\,W\,V_k\,W^T\,J_k^T
  \]
  and the screen-space kernel uses \(\hat V'_k\) (2D submatrix) and optional low-pass \(V_h\):
  \[
  \varphi_k(x)=c_k o_k \frac{1}{|W^{-1}J_k^{-1}|}\,G^2_{\hat V'_k+V_h}(x-x_k)
  \]
  - Supports: “a 3D Gaussian becomes a 2D ellipse/conic on screen under perspective; rasterization evaluates that ellipse efficiently.”

### Deployment/system numbers (3DGS telepresence + SLAM taxonomy)
- **Mon3tr telepresence (3DGS avatar) (2026):**
  - Offline amortization: **~33 s per user** from **1–2 min** multi-view RGB video.
  - Online bandwidth: **~0.2 Mbps** motion stream (also reported **~0.16 Mbps** end-to-end).
  - Receiver rendering: **60 FPS** on Meta Quest 3; end-to-end latency **73.1 ms** (also stated ~80 ms).
- **3DGS vs NeRF for real-time SLAM/telepresence (2025 taxonomy):**
  - 3DGS-SLAM: **3–30 FPS end-to-end**, but **rendering >100 FPS (up to 1000 FPS)**; examples include SplaTAM ~**400 FPS rendering**, Photo-SLAM **up to 1000 FPS rendering**.

### Sora Videos API constraints (OpenAI docs)
- **Async workflow:** `POST /v1/videos` → poll `GET /v1/videos/{id}` → download `GET /v1/videos/{id}/content` (URLs valid **≤1 hour**).
- **Durations:** **16s and 20s** generations.

## How It Works

### A. Text-to-video diffusion with temporal attention (Ho et al., 2022-style)
1. **Represent video as a tensor** (frames × height × width × channels) in pixel space or latent space.
2. **Forward diffusion:** sample a time \(t\), add Gaussian noise to get \(z_t\sim q(z_t|x)\).
3. **Denoiser network predicts noise (or \(x\))** using a **factorized space-time U-Net**:
   - Spatial processing: space-only 3D convs (e.g., \(1\times3\times3\)).
   - **Temporal attention blocks:** attend across frames (relative position embeddings).
4. **Training loss:** weighted MSE between predicted and true clean target (via \(\epsilon\)-prediction reconstruction).
5. **Sampling (reverse diffusion):** iteratively denoise from Gaussian noise to a video, optionally using:
   - **Classifier-free guidance** to strengthen conditioning.
   - **Reconstruction-guided sampling** when conditioning on known parts (e.g., extend a clip, super-res, inpaint) to avoid blockwise incoherence.

### B. Sora-style scaling idea (from OpenAI report framing)
1. **Compress raw video** into a lower-dimensional latent representation using a learned video compression network.
2. **Tokenize into spacetime patches** (patches are “visual tokens”).
3. **Train a transformer diffusion model** over patch tokens, jointly on images and videos of variable sizes/durations.
4. **Decode generated latents** back to pixel video via a decoder.

### C. NeRF rendering loop (what the tutor needs mid-conversation)
1. For each pixel, **generate a camera ray** from camera intrinsics/extrinsics.
2. **Sample depths** \(t_i\) along \([t_n,t_f]\) (often stratified bins).
3. Query NeRF MLP at each sample: \((\mathbf{c}_i,\sigma_i)=F_\theta(\mathbf{x}_i,\mathbf{d})\).
4. Convert densities to **alphas** \(\alpha_i=1-\exp(-\sigma_i\delta_i)\).
5. Compute **transmittance** \(T_i=\prod_{j<i}(1-\alpha_j)\).
6. Render pixel color \(\hat C=\sum_i T_i\alpha_i\mathbf{c}_i\).

### D. 3D Gaussian Splatting rendering loop (conceptual steps grounded in sources)
1. **Scene representation:** a set of Gaussians with \(\mu\) (center), \(\Sigma\) (covariance), opacity \(\alpha\), and color (often view-dependent).
2. **Project each Gaussian** through the camera; under perspective, it becomes a **screen-space ellipse/conic** (Zwicker 2002 math; 3DGS taxonomy card describes Jacobian-based projection).
3. **Rasterize splats**: evaluate Gaussian contribution over pixels within a threshold region (Zwicker notes typical \(r<c\), \(c=4\)).
4. **Depth-sort and alpha-composite** contributions to form the final image.
5. **Optimize parameters** via differentiable rendering; 3DGS pipelines often include **densification/pruning** (clone/split/prune) based on gradients/coverage (3DGS vs NeRF taxonomy card).

## Teaching Approaches

### Intuitive (no math): “Video = images + memory”
- Image diffusion can make a single frame look good.
- Video diffusion must also ensure *the same things stay the same* across frames (identity/background/style) while *motion changes smoothly*.
- Temporal attention is the model’s “memory across frames”: it lets frame \(t\) look at other frames to keep objects consistent and motion plausible.

### Technical (with math): “Same diffusion objective, bigger tensor + temporal attention”
- Training is still denoising: sample \(t\), noise \(z_t\), predict \(\epsilon_\theta(z_t)\), minimize MSE (Ho et al., Eq. 2).
- The architectural change is factorization: spatial convs handle per-frame structure; temporal attention handles cross-frame dependencies.
- For conditional extension/SR, replacement conditioning breaks coherence; reconstruction-guided sampling adds a gradient term (Ho et al., Eq. 8) that explicitly enforces agreement with the known part.

### Analogy-based: “Flipbook + editor”
- A video is a flipbook: if each page is drawn independently, the character’s face “jitters.”
- Temporal attention is the editor who checks adjacent pages to keep the character model consistent.
- VBench’s temporal metrics are like a rubric: “Does the character look like the same character?” (subject consistency), “Does the background stay stable?” (background consistency), “Do frames flicker?” (temporal flickering), “Is motion smooth?” (motion smoothness).

## Common Misconceptions

1. **“If each frame looks great, the video is great.”**  
   - **Why wrong:** frame-wise quality can be high while identity/background flicker across time; VBench explicitly separates **frame-wise quality** (aesthetic/imaging) from **temporal quality** (subject/background consistency, flicker, motion smoothness).  
   - **Correct model:** video quality is multi-dimensional; temporal consistency is a separate requirement that needs explicit modeling and evaluation (VBench/VBench++).

2. **“Temporal attention is just a 3D convolution.”**  
   - **Why wrong:** Ho et al. explicitly separate **space-only 3D convs** (e.g., \(1\times3\times3\)) from **temporal attention blocks** that attend across frames with relative position embeddings.  
   - **Correct model:** temporal attention is content-adaptive mixing across time; convolutions are fixed local filters.

3. **“To extend a video, you can just ‘replace’ the known frames and generate the rest.”**  
   - **Why wrong:** Ho et al. report replacement-style imputation yields severe incoherence; reconstruction-guided sampling dramatically improves FVD for long extensions (e.g., **451.45 → 136.22** at guidance weight 2.0).  
   - **Correct model:** conditioning must be enforced during sampling (e.g., via reconstruction-guided gradient terms), not only by hard replacement.

4. **“NeRF and 3D Gaussian Splatting are basically the same—both are neural rendering.”**  
   - **Why wrong:** NeRF renders via **ray sampling + MLP queries + volumetric integration**; 3DGS renders via **rasterization of explicit Gaussian primitives** and can reach real-time rendering rates (taxonomy card notes >100 FPS up to 1000 FPS rendering in some systems).  
   - **Correct model:** both are radiance-field-like, but their primitives and rendering algorithms differ (ray marching vs rasterization), driving different speed/memory trade-offs.

5. **“A world model is just a video generator.”**  
   - **Why wrong:** Ha & Schmidhuber define world models as generative models used to learn a compressed representation of an *environment* such that an agent can train policies using it—even inside hallucinated rollouts (“dreams”).  
   - **Correct model:** video generation can be a component/stepping stone, but “world model” implies agent-interaction utility (planning/learning), not only passive generation.

## Comparisons & Trade-offs

| Topic | Option A | Option B | Choose A when… | Choose B when… |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Video generation backbone | Factorized 3D U-Net + temporal attention (Ho et al., 2022) | Transformer over spacetime patches (Sora report) | You want a clear extension of U-Net diffusion with explicit temporal attention blocks; you’re studying classic video diffusion mechanics | You want scalable training across variable durations/resolutions/aspect ratios using tokenized patches (Sora framing) |
| Temporal coherence in conditional generation | Replacement/imputation conditioning | Reconstruction-guided sampling (Ho et al., Eq. 8) | Almost never for long/complex conditioning; known to cause incoherence | When extending/upsampling/conditioning and coherence matters; large FVD improvements reported |
| 3D scene representation | NeRF (implicit MLP + ray integration) | 3D Gaussian Splatting (explicit Gaussians + rasterization) | You want compact implicit representation pipelines; you’re focused on NeRF-style volumetric rendering math | You need real-time rendering / interactive systems; SLAM/telepresence pipelines where rasterization speed matters (3DGS taxonomy; Mon3tr) |
| Video evaluation | Single-number metrics (e.g., FVD) | Multi-dimension rubric (VBench/VBench++) | Quick coarse comparisons | Diagnosing *why* a model fails: flicker vs motion vs prompt adherence; human-aligned per-dimension judgments |

## Socratic Question Bank

1. **If a model’s frames look sharp but the subject “changes identity” every few frames, which VBench dimensions would likely flag it, and why?**  
   *Good answer:* subject consistency (DINO similarity), possibly temporal flickering; explains identity drift vs per-frame quality.

2. **In Ho et al.’s factorized architecture, what is the computational reason to separate spatial attention from temporal attention?**  
   *Good answer:* factorization reduces cost vs full space-time attention; temporal attention treats H×W as batch and attends over frames.

3. **Why does “replacement conditioning” create blockwise incoherence when extending videos? What does reconstruction guidance add?**  
   *Good answer:* replacement doesn’t enforce smooth compatibility at boundaries; reconstruction guidance adds gradient term toward matching known part (Eq. 8), improving FVD dramatically.

4. **How would you explain the difference between NeRF rendering and 3DGS rendering to someone who knows classic graphics rasterization?**  
   *Good answer:* NeRF = ray marching + integration; 3DGS = rasterize projected Gaussian ellipses + alpha composite (like triangles but Gaussian splats).

5. **What makes Sora’s “patch tokens” idea analogous to LLM tokens, according to the OpenAI report?**  
   *Good answer:* patches unify diverse visual data (images/videos of varying sizes) into a token sequence for transformer training.

6. **If you needed a real-time telepresence system on a headset, what evidence from the sources suggests 3DGS is a better fit than NeRF?**  
   *Good answer:* 3DGS rendering >100 FPS up to 1000 FPS in some systems; Mon3tr achieves 60 FPS on Quest 3 with low bandwidth.

7. **What’s the difference between “video generator” and “world model” in Ha & Schmidhuber’s sense?**  
   *Good answer:* world model is used for agent learning/planning; can train policy in hallucinated rollouts and transfer.

## Likely Student Questions

**Q: What exactly is “temporal attention” in video diffusion models?**  
→ **A:** In Ho et al. (2022), temporal attention is an attention block applied over the **frame dimension** (treating **H×W as batch**) with **relative position embeddings**, inserted into a factorized 3D U-Net; it can be **masked** so frames don’t mix, enabling joint image+video training.

**Q: How do we know replacement conditioning is bad for long video extension? Any numbers?**  
→ **A:** Ho et al. report that extending to **64 frames** from a 16-frame model gives **FVD 451.45** with replacement vs **136.22** with reconstruction-guided sampling at guidance weight 2.0 (and similarly **456.24 vs 133.92** at weight 5.0).

**Q: What does VBench measure for “temporal consistency”?**  
→ **A:** VBench/VBench++ decomposes temporal quality into **subject consistency** (DINO similarity across frames), **background consistency** (CLIP similarity), **temporal flickering** (mean absolute difference across static frames), **motion smoothness** (video interpolation model priors), and **dynamic degree** (RAFT optical flow magnitude).

**Q: What’s the core NeRF rendering equation in discrete form?**  
→ **A:** The MIT Vision Book gives the standard discrete approximation: sum over samples of transmittance \(\alpha_i\) times opacity \((1-e^{-\sigma_i\delta_i})\) times color \(c_i\):  
\(\ell(r)\approx \sum_i \alpha_i(1-e^{-L^\sigma(\mathbf R_i)\delta_i})L^c(\mathbf R_i,\mathbf D)\), with \(\alpha_i=\exp(-\sum_{j<i}L^\sigma(\mathbf R_j)\delta_j)\).

**Q: Why is 3D Gaussian Splatting faster for interactive systems than NeRF?**  
→ **A:** The 3DGS vs NeRF SLAM/telepresence taxonomy notes NeRF ray marching and slow convergence are poor for low-latency systems, while 3DGS uses differentiable **rasterization** and can achieve **rendering >100 FPS up to 1000 FPS** in some pipelines (though memory can be higher due to millions of primitives).

**Q: What are the hard constraints of the Sora Videos API (duration, extensions, characters)?**  
→ **A:** The OpenAI video generation guide states generations support **16s and 20s**; extensions add **≤20s** each, **≤6** times (max **120s** total), and **extensions cannot use characters or image references**. Character consistency uses `/v1/videos/characters`, supports **max 2 characters**, and you must mention the character name verbatim in the prompt.

**Q: How does Sora represent videos for training, according to OpenAI?**  
→ **A:** The OpenAI report says Sora compresses videos into a latent space, then decomposes them into **spacetime patches** that act as transformer tokens; this unifies images (1-frame videos) and videos of variable resolutions/durations/aspect ratios.
